Subject: How Teams work, standalone agents, and the vendor-organization model

Hi Jake,

Answers to your Team questions, grounded in how the system works today.


Q: A SINGLE ORGANIZATION CAN BE SPLIT INTO TEAMS. HOW DOES THIS WORK? WHAT OR
   WHO TRIGGERS IT?

Nothing triggers it automatically. Teams are opt-in and always set up by a
person, specifically an Admin. An Admin creates a team, names it, and assigns a
Team Lead. Until an Admin does that, the brokerage simply has no teams. From
there, the Admin (for any team) or that Team Lead (for their own team) adds
Agents and Coordinators to it, or an invitation can be aimed at a team so a new
hire lands on it the moment they join. So the "trigger" is always an Admin
deliberately building the structure, never something the system does on its own.


Q: IS A "TEAM" CONSIDERED ITS OWN ORGANIZATION? CAN THERE BE STANDALONE AGENTS
   INSIDE THE ORGANIZATION?

No, a Team is not its own organization. There is exactly one organization (the
brokerage), and Teams are just sub-groups inside it, like pods or branches under
one roof. Every team shares that same organization: one bill, one shared set of
data, and one Admin over all of them. A Team Lead only manages the people and
deals in their own pod, not the whole company.

And yes, standalone agents are fully supported. Being on a team is optional. An
agent or coordinator can belong to no team at all and still work normally. So a
brokerage can be completely flat with no teams, all pods, or a mix of pod
members and standalone agents. The organization is the container; teams are just
an optional way to group people inside it.


ON VENDOR ORGANIZATIONS AND THE REFERRAL / FEE-SPLIT MODEL

Yes, this makes sense, and no, I do not hate you. It is a clean, well-thought
model, and the good news is that the hardest part already exists.

Here is your idea in my own words, so we are sure I have it right: a vendor
company (a mortgage or title shop) is its own organization with several
individual vendors inside it (loan officers, sales reps). That vendor org can
offer Velvet Elves to agents at a reduced fee when there is a written agreement
in place. It hands an Agent, Team Lead, TC, FSBO, or Attorney a code that is
unique to the vendor org. When a transaction is started, the system checks
whether a vendor org is linked to it. If one is linked, that vendor org pays its
share of the fee; if none is linked, the agent covers the full fee. The vendor
org has to be attached to the transaction to owe anything, and each organization
sets its own billing and sales limits. Multiple vendors, one organization,
automatic link checks, and a paper trail for compliance.

Why the foundation is already there: the core building block you are describing,
an organization with multiple users, its own isolated data, its own roles and
seats, and its own billing, is exactly what the system is already built on.
Today that same primitive powers brokerages. A mortgage or title company would
be the same kind of organization, just a different flavor. So we are not
inventing "organizations" from scratch for this; we already have them.

What is genuinely new, and what I will protect the foundation for: right now
every organization is sealed off from every other one, on purpose. Your model
needs a controlled, auditable bridge between one organization's transaction and
another organization (the vendor), plus a fee-split that rides on that bridge.
That bridge does not exist yet, and it is the one piece I want to design
carefully so it is secure and compliant rather than bolted on later.

To keep the door open without building the whole thing now, here is what I will
bake into the foundation as we go:

- Treat vendor companies as real organizations, not just the single
  in-brokerage "Vendor" role we have today. That keeps the multi-user vendor org
  possible.

- Let a transaction record a first-class, auditable link to an outside vendor
  organization, along with the code that created the link. This is the hook the
  whole model hangs on.

- Keep billing per organization (its own plan, its own limits, its own payment
  account) so a fee can later be split or redirected based on that link. The
  payment rails for this are already in place.

- Log the written agreement and the code linkage as compliance evidence. The
  system already keeps an audit trail, so this fits naturally.

None of this has to be MVP. I just want the early decisions to point the right
way, so that when we build it the model slots in instead of forcing a rewrite.
Send me the monetization details whenever they are ready and I will line them up
against this.

from agents/brokerages/team leads. This monitoring could be a function of "AI

JAN:
Got it, and it fits cleanly.

The link between an agent and a vendor is many-to-many. One agent can hold many
codes at once, one per vendor they partner with (two title companies, three
lenders, and so on). Each code belongs to the vendor who issued it, so the
vendor can hand it out and pull it back whenever they want. The agent's side
just carries a list of their active codes.

Per deal, the agent picks which vendor applies to that transaction, and that
pick decides whose code and whose fee-split is in play. No vendor pays unless
their code is attached to that specific deal.

And yes, the monitoring you described is a natural job for AI Coaching later:
count how many deals each agent actually sends a vendor, report it back to the
vendor, and let them keep or revoke the code. I will model the data now so that
reporting is easy to switch on once AI Coaching is in scope.

This does not change the foundation. It just confirms the agent-to-vendor link
must be many-to-many with vendor-owned codes, which I will plan for.
